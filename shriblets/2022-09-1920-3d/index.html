or: something of a glorified blog post

and now for some artist commentary

why this drawing

The original was drawn on a whim, and turned into something of a style test. I’ve liked it for a while and, while there are some things I would draw differently nowadays (I think I would want to color the lineart more, the background + halo were afterthoughts, and the hair lacks contrast), it’s never been enough to justify an all-out redraw.

Recently I’ve been learning 3D via Blender, and (also on a whim) got the idea to remake one of my drawings with it. This one came to mind instantly.

The Process: modeling

All-out sculpting was intimidating and I had no plans to animate, so I went for a style inspired by Paint 3D. The workflow looks similar to how I do lineless art, with each part being its own object; I’ve messed with the program before but once you create a shape it’s tricky to modify, and it’s unintuitive to export for modification in other programs.

Blender + Solidify modifier (makes a flat plane look like a 3d surface) + Subdivision Surface modifier (makes a thing round) seemed viable—and, for the most part, it worked like a charm. Since the original drawing is fairly symmetrical (though, on close inspection, I’m not sure I actually used a symmetry tool) I “traced” it with the vertex points. (I didn’t screenshot the process, unfortunately, but you can see the raw wireframe later; it’s noticeably simpler than the curves shown above. Subsurface is magic.)

For the shadows, I swung the light overhead, pumped up the power, and applied Solidify (actually turned the planes into 3D). Then I messed with the Y-axis (pushing parts forward and back, essentially). Does the result make sense in actual 3D? Nah.

I thought, at one point, it might be neat to make the camera move a little bit, like those gifs where a flat picture looks 3D-ish through some photoshop magic. (Cannot find a good example for the life of me because I don’t know what you call that effect, but basically, imagine taking a picture, then shifting the camera back and forth a little.) But firstly, that would require working in orthographic perspective (I did not), and also then I got to the irregular parts.

For the hair, I followed this tutorial by YanSculpts. Translating this stylized hair to full 3D is gonna be a heck of a challenge, but since I only care about the front angle here, it was pretty fun to wing it.

I used the same technique for the neck-teeth since I wanted them to have a little bend and didn’t feel like fussing with cone geometry.

(For the actual neck cutout (not shown), I made the shape with a pitch black texture, overlapped it with the neck (and torso) objects, and used a Boolean modifier to “subtract” it. The shading is a little weird in the final render because the cutout has way more vertices than the neck itself, but I didn’t want to boost the neck subdivision because my computer doesn’t like high-poly models. But eh, I was also too lazy to model the neck veiny bits, so I don’t think it sticks out too badly….)

The halo is just a couple of toruses (donut shapes) and some cylinders for the glow lines, all with an Emission material. Considered animating this but I’m still trying to get a handle on parenting and transformations; essentially, things were being weird, opted not to.

Have some final models at suboptimal angles.

Other bits of note:

  • The face fought me so bad. I don’t have a super 3D way of drawing faces in the first place, so rather than try to model it all in one go, I took a potato head approach and slapped the nose and mouth on top—which made me sorely aware that I need to practice the mouth and eye areas. I also didn’t quite indent the head enough to make the eye shadow curve where it does in the drawing, but ah well, the bangs probably would’ve thrown that off anyway.
    • Noses I’m a lot more comfortable with, though the model here turned out wider than strictly intended. Also had to turn off the shadow for it because otherwise it covered the whole mouth/chin area. (It was either that or make it flatter and lose the highlight.) Had the same shadow issue with the mouth, since the chin’s pretty flat… ah well, stuff to keep in mind for future projects.
  • The sleeve cuffs were simultaneously a blessing (could BS the wrist area more) and a curse (oh my god, getting it to wrap). The (viewer’s) left one follows the drawing contours much, much more closely, and it’s got a weird highlight because it sticks forward a ton. The right one is flatter/closer to the actual arm, but at the sacrifice of shape.

The Process: materials & lighting

Don’t have a lot of screenshots for this one, but essentially, I do not understand the Principled BSDF shader but watched this a bunch and am trying my best. Also I had a small field day with emission gradients, though it only looks good on the hair; don’t quite know why it fades the way it does on the non-hair parts but oh well.

For the most part, I tried to use the same colors as the original drawing, including the background gradient, but with some newer liberties for hair contrast. And then more liberties to make the shadows in general look better. And then I added a bunch more lights because why not.

The background was surprisingly tricky; I didn’t want to “cheat” and use an image, but I also made the original by just drawing with a low-opacity same-size brush and messing with layer modes, which is not exactly an option in Blender.

What I landed on was sticking a big area light underneath the background plane, then uing a jagged invisible object to mess with shadows. (I don’t know why a see-through object casts shadows but hey, it works here!) It’s not as intricate as the original but makes a simile effect, so #GoodEnough.

The Finished Product

No lineart because, frankly, I was too tired at this point to figure it out. Remaking the original drawing 1:1 would’ve been very difficult if not impossible; some changes (hair contrast, darker background) were intentional, others were a byproduct of not thinking it through more or just not wanting to mess with it more (some hand shading, neck detail). But all things considered, I’m pretty dang happy with this!

…And that’s about all I have to say, considering this page was 90% process rambling :D I don’t even have any special commentary on the piece itself; I’m agnostic, I’m not religious, but I am not immune to thinking certain little bits of symbology look cool. Half the reason this blog post of a page exists at all is because I’ve been thinking of making monthly art roundups via code, and so now this is an experiment of that sort too.

Anyway, on to the next project!
